WOLFGANG WEINGART

Introduction

Wolfgang Weingart is a well known graphic designer and typographer. He is also known as 'the father' of the 'new wave' typography. He gained popularity and exposure for his experimental work with traditional Swiss typography.

Early Life

Weingart was born in February 1941, in Salem Valley, Southern Germany close to the Swiss border.

In April 1954 he moved Lisbon, Portugal and lived there for two years, Wolfgang describes this time as; "The most important years of my life". There he enrolled in the German School of Lisbon and his artistic skills were noticed by his teacher who provided him with private lessons so that he could develop them further. Lisbon was also a quality time in his life that he has spent with his parents, they took him on many trips during the holidays visiting different places but in particular the Arabic countries which gave him a better cultural and visual knowledge. These experiences would then inspire his works and designs later on in life.

When he returned back to Germany his parents enrolled him for the Merz Academy in Stuttgart where he started a two year programme in Applied Art and Design. Learning the art of production by designing and sketching designs using different mediums. He also worked on personal projects in his free time which included hand setting type and print, during these projects he first used the metal type.

Apprenticeships

In 1960 Weingart started a three year typesetting apprenticeship at Ruwe Printing. During this time he came to contact with Swiss typography. In these years he has mastered techniques of letterpress printing and wanted to experiment his newly gained skills. Weingart experimented with letters and type their positioning and compositions. The experimenting lead to unusual letter spacing and duplication of type on pages. There he met with the company’s consulting designer, Karl-August Hanke, who became his mentor and encouraged him to study in Switzerland. In 1962 he decided to work with only the letter 'M', this project would help him through many years to come as it questioned; size and positioning of type on pages and how they would look from different perspectives. It was curiosity that influenced him to combine different type with the letter 'M' to produce different patters and designs. He gained the knowledge to manipulate the letters by duplicating, bending and twisting the work within the pages.

After he had completed the apprenticeship at Ruwe Printing in March 1963, he moved to Basel, Switzerland where he enrolled in the Basel School of Design. There he met Armin Hofmann which was impressed by the skills and work of Weingart. In his book 'My Way to Typography' he mentions; "Hofmann's enthusiasm led me to believe that I would be accepted as a student". At Basel he was taught by Armin Hofmann and Emil Ruder, they have aided his experimental work of type by providing him access to large work spaces within the school. Weingart would decide to work at Ruwe Printing whilst studying to investigate lino and woodcut illustrations as he wanted to improve the newly acquired Swiss style.

Swiss Typography

It was founded during World War II at the Bauhaus but In the 1950's there was a design movement that is seen by many people as the most important graphic design style of the twentieth century. I was mostly used in Switzerland, Russia and Germany, and although mostly known as the Swiss Typography it is also known as the 'International typographic style' due to its global recognition. The style became to be very much connected with corporate and political design with the emphasize on being eye-catching and readable. This was because a lot of political posters have used the style. It is also fairly known for its basic use of colours and simplicity.

Admin Hogmann and Emil Ruder Weingarts teachers and biggest influences were enthusiasts of the Swiss Typography but Weingart was not much of a fan of the structured and basic design that is why he experimented. Weingarts process was unusual as he took slabs of wood and screwed metal hooks into them creating a grid format that was common in the Swiss designs, then he would have turned some of the hooks in a 45 and others to a 90 degree angle to form different patterns and compositions. Later he would ink them and print them on a letterpress, as this was the process he was most comfortable with as he worked as a typesetter.

Work

Weingart decided to finish his project he had started few years prior. This was known as the 'M Project', he stretched, duplicated, angled, printed and pasted the letter onto a cube. Then he proceeded to photograph the letter from different angles, this lead to many abstract compositions and versions of the letter. This was done in back and white for easy visibility of the compositions and patterns that were created in the process. He said that most of his work reminded him of murals and sculptures he witnessed at a young age when he traveled with his parents.

The so called 'new wave' work included poster, as Weingart loved the combination of type and photography and fell in love with photo montage. The experimental work was a mixture of typefaces, letter spacing and photos. He used offset printing processes to create these poster that appeared both complex and chaotic but every much attracted the eye of a viewer with their colours and originality. Later on these posters became his most famous and beloved works as they were something unseen and conventional. When creating these posters Weingart was inspired by Dada collage which was produced by layering type and images on top of one another to be then photographed to create one single image.

One piece of work that was truly admired by others was the 1984 exhibition poster of the 'Matterhorn', he got his inspiration by crumpling up tissue forcing it onto a page and photocopying it, this was seen as sheer brilliance as it gave an effect not seen before at that time. It's his thinking outside the box and experimentation that attracted many people to his work.

Teaching

In 1968, he began to teach typography at Basel School of Design, where he previously attended after Armin Hofmanns invitation after completion of the course. He began to teach Advance Program for Graphic Design, he left Basel in 2005 after gaining life time popularity and became an instructor at the Yale University Summer Graphic Design program in Brissago, Switzerland. He continued to lecture all around the globe, in places such as Australia, Asia, Europe and North and South America for 40 years. He has provided a lot of knowledge for the upcoming designers in the industry.

He encouraged all of his students to experiment with the same goal to test the limits of readability of their type work not just relations and combinations between type positioning, size, weight and letter spacing. All of the students were allowed creative freedom and experimentation as Weingart wanted to push the limits of 'the norm' that he was taught. Because of this students got very familiar with this type of 'style' and dubbed it 'Weingart style' also known as the 'New Wave'.

"I took 'Swiss Typography' as my starting point, but then I blew it apart, never forcing any style upon my students. I never intended to create a 'style'. It just happened that the students picked up — and misinterpreted — a so-called 'Weingart style' and spread it around.”

Awards

Over the years Wolfgang Weingart has received some worthy awards; In 2005 MassArt awarded Weingart the honorary title of Doctor of Fine Arts. In 2013 Weingart was a recipient of the AIGA Medal, which is the highest honor of the design profession. He recieved it for his typographic explorations and teaching in the industry. In 2014 Weingart received the Swiss Grand Prix of Design award, presented by the Federal Office of Culture for his lifelong merits as a designer.

Conclusion

Weingart had a great understanding of typesetting and didn't stick to 'the norm' of design, this led him to pursue a more experimental approach to his designs. Weingart has a lot to thank for Swiss typography that was around his time of come up as it was because of Swiss typography he became an international success in the 1960's. Weingart wanted a whole new perspective on the teaching of typography by looking at was the common principles of its practice and removing them for unlimited creativity in design. He broke the rules because he knew them inside out.

I don't think Weingart intended for his style to become this popular, he was just doing something personally for himself but other saw it as something great.

He had a huge impact on graphic design industry as he helped to break apart 'the norms' that were set by others previously and if you see him as a typographer, a graphic designer, a teacher, an inventor, the father of 'New Wave' or just a rebel and deserves to be named one of the greatest in his craft.

"For me, typography is a triangular relationship between design idea, typographic elements, and printing technique"- Wolfgang Weingart.
